Case **(2)**: If $k\neq 0$, Then we get that $k\in\{1,...,n\}$, We’ll prove that $\forall i\in\{1,...,k\},0\lt\frac{n-i+1}{n}\leq 1$: Let $i\in\{1,...,k\}$, Then we have $1\leq i\leq k$, But since $k\in\{1,...,n\}$, We have $k\leq n$, And thus we can conclude that $1\leq i\leq n$, And by multiplying this inequality by $-1$, We get $-n\leq -i\leq -1$, And by adding $n$ to this inequality, We get $0=n-n\leq n-i \leq n-1$, And now after adding $1$ to the inequality, We get **(§)** $1\leq n-i+1\leq n$, Now since $n\in\mathbb{N}$, We get that $ n\gt 0$, And thus by dividing the inequality **(§)** by $n\gt 0$, We conclude that $\frac{1}{n}\leq\frac{n-i+1}{n}\leq\frac{n}{n}=1$, But since $0\lt n$, We get that $0\lt\frac{1}{n}$, And thus we can conclude that $0\lt\frac{n-i+1}{n}\leq 1$ as was to be shown. Now, Since we have $\forall i\in\{1,...,k\},0\lt\frac{n-i+1}{n}\leq 1$, We can conclude that $0<\prod_\limits{i=1}^k\frac{n-i+1}{n}\leq 1^k=1$, But since $\prod_\limits{i=1}^k\frac{n-i+1}{n}=\frac{\prod_\limits{i=1}^k (n-i +1)}{\prod_\limits{i=1}^k n}=\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k}$, We conclude that $0\lt \frac{P(n,k)}{n^k}\leq 1$. Thus from cases **(1)** and **(2)** we see that is always the case that $0\lt\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k}\leq 1$ as was to be shown. Now we’ll prove that: **(fact 2)** $\forall n\in\mathbb{N},\forall k\in\{0,...,n\},0\lt \frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k}\leq\frac{1}{k!}$ Let $n\in\mathbb{N}$ and $k\in\{0,...,n\}$, By **(fact 1)** we get **(§)** $0\lt \frac{P(n,k)}{n^k}\leq 1$, And by using the fact $k!\gt 0$, We get by dividing **(§)** by $k!\gt 0$ that $0\lt \frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k}\leq\frac{1}{k!}$ as was to be shown. And now we’ll prove that: **(fact 3)** $\forall n\in\mathbb{N}, 0\lt \sum_\limits{k=0}^n (\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k})\leq\sum_\limits{k=0}^n\frac{1}{k!}$ Let $n\in\mathbb{N}$, By **(fact 2)** we get that $\forall k\in\{0,...,n\},0\lt \frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k}\leq\frac{1}{k!}$, And thus we can conclude by summing all of those inequalities together that $0=0\cdot (n+1)=\sum_\limits{k=0}^n 0\lt \sum_\limits{k=0}^n(\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k})\leq\sum_\limits{k=0}^n\frac{1}{k!}$ as was to be shown. Now we’ll prove by induction that: **(fact 4)** $\forall n\in\mathbb{N}, 2^{n-1}\leq n!$: For $n=1$, We have to show that $2^{n-1}\leq n!$: Since $2^{n-1}=2^{1-1}=2^0=1$, And since $n!=1!=1$, We conclude that $2^{n-1}\leq n!$. Now suppose that for some $n=k\in\mathbb{N}$, We have $2^{k-1}\leq k!$, And we’ll prove that for $n=k+1$ we have $2^{(k+1)-1}=(k+1)!$: Since $k\in\mathbb{N}$, We conclude that $1\leq k$, And thus $0\lt 2\leq k+1$, But since $2^{k-1}\leq k!$ (this is the induction hypothesis), We get by multiplying this inequality by $k+1\gt 0$ that $(k+1)2^{k-1}\leq (k+1)k!=(k+1)!$, Now by multiplying the inequality $2\leq k+1$ by $2^{k-1}>0$, We get that $2^k= 2\cdot 2^{k-1}\leq (k+1)2^{k-1}$, But since we’ve already shown that $(k+1)2^{k-1}\leq(k+1)!$, We conclude that $2^k\leq (k+1)!$, But because $2^{(k+1)-1}=2^k$, We conclude that $2^{(k+1)-1}\leq (k+1)!$ as was to be shown and the proof by induction is completed. Now we’ll prove that: **(fact 5)** $\forall n\in\mathbb{N}, \frac{1}{n!}\leq\frac{1}{2^{n-1}}$: Let $n\in\mathbb{N}$, Since $2^{n-1}\gt 0$ and since $n!\gt 0$, We can conclude that $2^{n-1}\cdot n!\gt 0$, Also by **(fact 4)** we get that $2^{n-1}\leq n!$, And thus by dividing this inequality by $2^{n-1}\cdot n!\gt 0$, We conclude that $\frac{1}{n!}=\frac{2^{n-1}}{2^{n-1}\cdot n!}\leq \frac{n!}{2^{n-1}\cdot n!}=\frac{1}{2^{n-1}}$ as was to be shown. And now we’ll prove that: **(fact 6)** $\forall n\in\mathbb{N}, \sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{k!}\leq \sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{2^{k-1}}=2(1-\frac{1}{2^n})<2$: Let $n\in\mathbb{N}$, By **(fact 5)** we get $\forall k\in\mathbb{N},\frac{1}{k!}\leq\frac{1}{2^{k-1}}$, And because $\{1,...,n\}\subseteq \mathbb{N}$, We get that in particular $\forall k\in\{1,...,n\}, \frac{1}{k!}\leq\frac{1}{2^{k-1}}$, And thus we can conclude by summing all of those inequalities together that *(§)* $\sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{k!}\leq\sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{2^{k-1}}$. Now let’s define a geometric sequence $\{g_m\in\mathbb{R}\}_{m=1}^\infty$, By the rules $g_1:=1$ and $\forall m\in\mathbb{N},g_m:=g_1 \cdot q^{m-1}$, Now we’ll show that $\forall m\in\mathbb{N}, g_m=\frac{1}{2^{m-1}}$: Let $m\in\mathbb{N}$, Then $g_m=g_1\cdot q^{n-1}=1\cdot (\frac{1}{2})^{n-1}=\frac{1}{2^{n-1}}$ as was to be shown. Thus we get by the forumula for sum of geometric sequence (see *Beni Goren, Algebra, Book 4, Page 91*) that $$\sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{2^{n-1}}=\sum_\limits{k=1}^n g_k=\frac{g_1(q^n-1)}{q-1}=\frac{1\cdot ((\frac{1}{2})^n-1)}{\frac{1}{2}-1}=\frac{\frac{1}{2^n}-1}{-\frac{1}{2}}=\frac{1-\frac{1}{2^n}}{\frac{1}{2}}=2(1-\frac{1}{2^n})$$ Now since $0\lt 2^n$, We get $0\lt\frac{1}{2^n}$ And thus $-\frac{1}{2^n}\lt 0$, And by adding $1$, We conclude that $1-\frac{1}{2^n}\lt 1$, And by multiplying by $2$, We conclude that $2(1-\frac{1}{2^n})\lt 2$. In summary we got that $ \sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{k!}\leq \sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{2^{k-1}}=2(1-\frac{1}{2^n})\lt 2$ as was to be shown. Now we’ll prove that: **(fact 7)** $\forall n\in\mathbb{N},\sum_\limits{k=0}^n\frac{1}{k!}\lt 3$ Let $n\in\mathbb{N}$, By **(fact 6)** we get that $\sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{k!}\lt 2$, And thus we can conclude that $1+\sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{k!}\lt 3$, But since $n\in\mathbb{N}$, We get that $n\geq 1$, And thus we can write $\sum_\limits{k=0}^n\frac{1}{k!}=\frac{1}{0!}+\sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{k!}=\frac{1}{1}+\sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{k!}=1+\sum_\limits{k=1}^n\frac{1}{k!}$, And we conclude that $\sum_\limits{k=0}^n\frac{1}{k!}\lt 3$ as was to be shown. And now we can prove that: **(fact 8)** $\forall n\in\mathbb{N}, 2\leq a_n\lt 3$ Let $n\in\mathbb{N}$, Then we get by definition of the sequence $\{a_n\in\mathbb{R}\}_{n=1}^\infty$ and by using the *binomial theorem* that: $a_n=(1+\frac{1}{n})^n=(\frac{1}{n}+1)^n=\sum_\limits{k=0}^n\binom{n}{k}(\frac{1}{n})^k\cdot 1^{n-k}=\sum_\limits{k=0}^n\binom{n}{k}\frac{1}{2^k}=\sum_\limits{k=0}^n(\frac{P(n,k)}{k!}\cdot\frac{1}{n^k})=\sum_\limits{k=0}^n(\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k})$ Now by **(fact 3)** we get that $\sum_\limits{k=0}^n (\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k})\leq\sum_\limits{k=0}^n\frac{1}{k!} $, Also by **(fact 7)** we get that $\sum_\limits{k=0}^n\frac{1}{k!}\lt 3$, And thus we can conclude that $a_n\lt 3$. Now, Since $n\in\mathbb{N}$, We get that there are two cases **(1)** $n=1$ or **(2)** $n\geq 2$: Case **(1)**: If $n=1$, Then we have $a_n=\sum_\limits{k=0}^n(\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k})=\sum_\limits{k=0}^1(\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(1,k)}{1^k})=\frac{1}{0!}\cdot\frac{P(1,0)}{1^0}+\frac{1}{1!}\cdot\frac{P(1,1)}{1^1}=\frac{1}{1}\cdot\frac{1}{1}+\frac{1}{1}\cdot\frac{1}{1}=1+1=2$, And thus we conclude that $2\leq a_n$. Case **(2)**: If $n\geq 2$, Then we have **(§)** $a_n=\sum_\limits{k=0}^n(\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k})=\frac{1}{0!}\cdot\frac{P(n,0)}{n^0}+\frac{1}{1!}\cdot\frac{P(n,1)}{n^1}+\sum_\limits{k=2}^n(\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k})=\frac{1}{1}\cdot\frac{1}{n^0}+\frac{1}{1}\cdot\frac{n}{n^1}+\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k}=2+\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k}$, Now by **(fact 2)** we get that $\forall k\in\{0,...,n\},0\lt \frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k}$, And thus we have in particular that $\forall k\in\{2,...,n\},0\lt \frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k}$, And by summing together all of those inequalities we can conclude that $0=\sum_\limits{k=2}^n0\lt \sum_\limits{k=2}^n (\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k})$, And by adding $2$ to this inequality we get that $2\lt 2+\sum_\limits{k=2}^n (\frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{P(n,k)}{n^k})$, And by **(§)** we conclude that $2\lt a_n$, And thus in particular $2\leq a_n$. Therefore we see from cases **(1)** and **(2)** that it is always the case that $2\leq a_n$. Thus we got that $a_n\lt 3$ and that $2\leq a_n$, And we conclude that $2\leq a_n\lt 3$ as was to be shown.
