Let $q\in\mathbb{N}$ be some natural number and let $\mathbb{F}_q:=\{\lambda_1,\lambda_2,...,\lambda_q\}$ be an alphabet with $q$ distinct symbols (I.e. $|\mathbb{F}_q|=q$). Let’s define a function $neq :\mathbb{F}_q\times\mathbb{F}_q\to\{0,1\}$ such that: $$\forall x,y\in\mathbb{F}_q,neq(x,y):= \begin{cases} 0 & x=y \\ 1 & x\neq y \end{cases} $$ We’ll prove the following propositions: **(Prop.1)** $\forall x,y\in\mathbb{F}_q, neq(x,y)\in\mathbb{Z}\land 0\leq neq(x,y)\leq 1$ **(Prop.2)** $\forall x,y\in\mathbb{F}_q, neq(x,y)=0\leftrightarrow x=y$ **(Prop.3)** $\forall x,y\in\mathbb{F}_q, neq(x,y)=neq(y,x)$ **(Prop.4)** $\forall x,y,z\in\mathbb{F}_q, neq(x,y)\leq neq(x,z)+neq(z,y)$ ---------- **Proof of (Prop.1):** Let $x,y\in\mathbb{F}_q$, Then by definition of $neq$, We get that $neq(x,y)\in\{0,1\}$, And thus $neq(x,y)\in\mathbb{Z}$ and $0\leq neq(x,y) \leq 1$ as was to be shown. Q.E.D. **Proof of (Prop.2):** Let $x,y\in\mathbb{F}_q$, We’ll show that $neq(x,y)=0 \Longleftrightarrow x=y$: $\Longrightarrow$ Suppose $neq(x,y)=0$, Then we get that it must be the case that $x=y$, Because if we suppose by contradiction that $x\neq y$, Then by definition of $neq$, We would get that $neq(x,y)=1$ which contradicts the fact that $neq(x,y)=0$. Thus it must be the case that $x=y$ as was to be shown. $\Longleftarrow$ Suppose $x=y$, Then by definition of $neq$, We get that $neq(x,y)=0$ as was to be shown. Q.E.D. **Proof of (Prop.3):** Let $x,y\in\mathbb{F}_q$, Then there are two cases **(Case.1)** $x=y$ or **(Case.2)** $x\neq y$: **(Case.1)** If $x=y$, Then we get by the symmetric property of the $=$ relation, That it must be also the case that $y=x$, And thus we get by definition of $neq$, That $neq(x,y)=0$ and $neq(y,x)=0$, And we conclude that $neq(x,y)=neq(y,x)$. **(Case.2)** If $x\neq y$, Then we get by the symmetric property of the $\neq$ relation, That it must be also the case that $y\neq x$, And thus we get by definition of $neq$, That $neq(x,y)=1$ and $neq(y,x)=1$, And we conclude that $neq(x,y)=neq(y,x)$. Thus we see from cases **(Case.1)** and **(Case.2)** that it is always the case that $neq(x,y)=neq(y,x)$ as was to be shown. Q.E.D. **Proof of (Prop.4):** Let $x,y,z\in\mathbb{F}_q$, Then there are two cases **(Case.1)** $x=y$ or **(Case.2)** $x\neq y$: **(Case.1)** If $x=y$, Then we get by definition of $neq$, That $neq(x,y)=0$, Also by **(Prop.1)** we have $0\leq neq(x,z)$ and $0\leq neq(z,y)$, And thus, We can conclude that $neq(x,y)=0=0+0\leq neq(x,z)+neq(z,y)$. **(Case.2)** If $x\neq y$, Then we get by definition of $neq$, That $neq(x,y)=1$, Now there are two cases **(Case.2.1)** $x=z$ or **(Case.2.2)** $x\neq z$: **(Case.2.1)** If $x=z$, Then we get by definition of $neq$, That $neq(x,z)=0$, Also by the fact that $x\neq y$, We get that it is also the case that $z\neq y$, And thus we get by definition of $neq$, That $neq(z,y)=1$, And so $neq(x,y)=1\leq 1=0+1=neq(x,z)+neq(z,y)$. **(Case.2.2)** If $x\neq z$, The we get by definition of $neq$, That $neq(x,z)=1$, Also since by **(Prop.1)** we have $0\leq neq(z,y)$, We conclude that $neq(x,y)=1= 1+0\leq neq(x,z)+neq(z,y)$. Therefore we conclude from cases **(Case.1)** and **(Case.2)** that it is always the case that $neq(x,y)\leq neq(x,z)+neq(z,y)$ as was to be shown. Q.E.D. ---------- Now let $n\in\mathbb{N}$ be some natural number and let’s define a function $d:\mathbb{F}_q^n\times\mathbb{F}_q^n\to\mathbb{R}$ as follows: $\forall \vec{x},\vec{y}\in\mathbb{F}_q^n, d(\vec{x},\vec{y}):=\sum\limits_{i=1}^n neq(x_i,y_i)$ We’ll prove the following propositions: **(Prop.5)** $\forall \vec{x},\vec{y}\in\mathbb{F}_q^n, d(\vec{x},\vec{y})\in\mathbb{Z}\land 0\leq d(\vec{x},\vec{y})\leq n$ **(Prop.6)** $\forall \vec{x},\vec{y}\in\mathbb{F}_q^n, d(\vec{x},\vec{y})=0\leftrightarrow \vec{x}=\vec{y}$ **(Prop.7)** $\forall \vec{x},\vec{y}\in\mathbb{F}_q^n, d(\vec{x},\vec{y})= d(\vec{y},\vec{x})$ **(Prop.8)** $\forall \vec{x},\vec{y},\vec{z}\in\mathbb{F}_q^n, d(\vec{x},\vec{y})\leq d(\vec{x},\vec{z})+ d(\vec{z},\vec{y})$ ---------- **Proof of (Prop.5):** Let $\vec{x},\vec{y}\in\mathbb{F}_q^n$, Then by definition of $d$, We get that $d(\vec{x},\vec{y})=\sum\limits_{i=1}^n neq(x_i,y_i)$, Now by **(Prop.1)** we have $\forall i\in\{1,...,n\}, d(x_i,y_i)\in\mathbb{Z}$, And since $\mathbb{Z}$ is closed under addition, We get that $\sum\limits_{i=1}^n neq(x_i,y_i)\in\mathbb{Z}$, And thus $d(\vec{x},\vec{y})\in\mathbb{Z}$. Also by **(Prop.1)** we have $\forall i \in\{1,...,n\}, 0\leq neq(x_i,y_i)\leq 1$, And thus, By summing all of those inequalities together we get that $0=n\cdot 0=\sum\limits_{i=1}^n 0\leq\sum\limits_{i=1}^n neq(x_i,y_i)\leq \sum\limits_{i=1}^n 1=n\cdot 1=n$, And thus $0\leq d(\vec{x},\vec{y})\leq n$ as was to be shown.
